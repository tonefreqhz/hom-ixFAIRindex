Cash purchases as a proportion of total transactions — interpretation
========================================================================

What this metric is:
- Cash share (%) = cash purchases / (cash purchases + mortgage purchases), quarterly.
  Higher values mean a larger fraction of sales are not using a mortgage.

What changes in cash share usually suggest:
- If cash share rises (up):
  - Mortgages may be harder/less attractive (higher rates, tighter lending, affordability squeeze).
  - Cash-rich buyers can dominate: downsizers, investors, corporate/portfolio buyers, or overseas buyers.
  - In downturns, mortgage-dependent demand often falls faster than cash demand, pushing the share up.
- If cash share falls (down):
  - Mortgage credit is more available/cheaper (lower rates, looser lending, improved sentiment).
  - First-time buyer and mortgaged mover activity is stronger relative to cash buyers.

What your most recent movement implies (based on 1-year change):
- England: cash share is falling over 1y (-0.66 pp).
  That leans toward improving mortgage accessibility/costs, or a rebound in mortgaged buyers.
- Wales: cash share is rising over 1y (+1.07 pp).
  Often seen when mortgage demand is softer than cash demand, or cash-rich segments are more active.

Caveats (worth keeping you honest):
- This is a *share*: it can rise even if cash purchases are flat, as long as mortgage purchases fall.
- Cash vs mortgage is only one slice; it doesn’t tell you whether buyers are investors vs owner-occupiers.
- If totals differ from new+existing counts, treat mix comparisons cautiously (the dataset can have quirks).
